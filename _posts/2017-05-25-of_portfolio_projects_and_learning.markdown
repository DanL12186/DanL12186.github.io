I am at this point more or less finished with my Rails Portfolio Project. I'm sure there are more errors to discover and stamp out, and there are of course *always* more features one might add, but I feel it's done. 

My main, unsurprising regret is that I took basically an entire week off before really beginning it, so that by this point it's been nearly two weeks since I finished the Flatiron Store Project. 

I say I took a week off-- and I did, from Learn, with the exception of building the skeleton with Rails new and generating a few files, which took all of ten minutes. This does not mean, however, that I took much time off from learning. Every time I "take a break" from coding (usually only a couple of hours), I tend to find myself reading over pages on StackOverflow on Rails-related questions, reading blog posts on topics related to what I've been working on, or venturing back to Codewars. This is despite my best efforts and not at all on purpose.

I suppose in some ways this is unsurprising; when you're doing one thing 8-12 hours a day, it tends to take over your headspace. I find myself wondering about answers to questions I've had about things related to what I've been doing, but haven't felt I had the time to dive into when I've been trying to progress in the Learn cirriculum. In many ways this is a very good thing: I find time to learn some of the things I felt I needed to (or just get a better grasp on), and it's simultaneously relaxing.

In a similar way, Portfolio Projects are kind of relaxing for me. There's no feeling of being rushed. It's not expected that you'll finish it in a day, and so there's no frustration or uneasiness involved when things take a bit longer than expected. And in my experience, they are periods of learning new concepts and truly solidifying old ones. 

Granted, it's not all a cakewalk; I do find it hard sometimes to get clear information from Google on certain subjects (I'm looking at you, custom attr_writer for nested forms). But by and large, there are a lot of "*OH!*" moments. Times when you finally really get it, and everything falls into place. And that, for me (and I imagine just about anybody), is both incredibly important and satisfying. You could certainly get by with only a cursory understanding of certain subjects, but any time something went really wrong, you'd be in trouble. And given that in general, any time you're working for a company, the code you're working on falls into the "something went wrong" category, this isn't a great way to approach programming. 

So I'd just like to put it out there that I love Portfolio projects.
